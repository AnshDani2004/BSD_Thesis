% ABSTRACT
%---------------------------------------------------------------
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Sequential resource allocation under uncertainty is a canonical problem in both
probability theory and financial mathematics. Classical strategies such as the
Kelly Criterion achieve asymptotically optimal log-wealth growth in stationary
environments, yet fail catastrophically when market regimes shift due to
\emph{sticky prior} effects---the tendency of Bayesian estimators to over-weight
historical evidence against rapidly changing conditions.

This thesis develops a rigorous simulation framework to study these failure modes
and proposes two complementary remedies. First, a \emph{Volatility-Augmented
Hidden Markov Model (Vol-HMM)} that augments standard return observations with a
rolling volatility signal and employs a CUSUM change-point trigger, reducing
empirically measured regime-detection lag from approximately 15--20 steps to
under 2 steps. Second, a \emph{Risk-Constrained Kelly (CPPI)} agent that
enforces a trailing maximum drawdown floor, guaranteeing---in the class of tested
scenarios---zero ruin events while quantifying the exact \emph{cost of survival}
in terms of foregone compounded annual growth.

We evaluate eight agents across three synthetic market regimes (stationary, slow
drift, adversarial shock) and four empirical S\&P~500 epochs (2000 Dot-Com, 2008
Global Financial Crisis, 2020 COVID crash, 2013--2019 Bull market). Statistical
significance is established via Fisher's Exact Test for ruin frequency and the
Mann-Whitney U Test for terminal wealth distributions. Transaction cost
sensitivity analyses demonstrate that high-turnover agents (EXP3, UCB) suffer
net wealth erosion at 25--50 basis points of friction, while the Vol-HMM's
regime-aware allocation reduces unnecessary rebalancing and preserves net
geometric growth.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Kelly criterion, Bayesian sequential decisions,
multi-armed bandits, Hidden Markov Models, CPPI, regime detection, heavy tails,
non-stationary environments.
